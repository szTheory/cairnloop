# Cairnloop Brand Book

Version: 0.1 working system  
Date: 2026-05-10  
Audience: humans, LLM agents, designers, developers, documentation writers, and OSS maintainers  
Status: creative direction, not legal clearance

## 0. LLM capsule

Cairnloop is an open-source Phoenix-native embedded support automation library for solo SaaS founders and small engineering teams. It lives inside the host app, uses host-owned data and Ecto state, and turns customer conversations into sourced answers, safe handoffs, knowledge-base improvements, and product signals.

Use **Cairnloop** in prose. Use `:cairn_loop` for the Hex package. Use `CairnLoop` for Elixir modules. Do not write "CairnLoop" in marketing prose unless referring to code.

The primary tagline is: **Support that leaves a trail.**

Cairnloop is not a generic helpdesk, not a chatbot, not an AI agent SaaS, not a CRM, and not an Intercom/Zendesk clone. It is an embedded support-knowledge loop for Phoenix apps: app-context-aware, sourced, policy-gated, auditable, and calm.

Cairnloop should feel like a careful trail guide plus an operator-grade reliability tool. Its copy should be clear, grounded, helpful, and specific. Avoid hype, bot language, exaggerated autonomy, and enterprise support jargon.

The visual system is warm stone, lichen, trailpaper, basalt, and copper - not bright SaaS blue, neon AI green, purple gradients, chat bubbles, or call-center stock imagery.

## 1. Strategic foundation

### 1.1 Brand idea

A cairn is a human-made stone marker: small, durable, and useful because it helps the next traveler stay on the path. Cairnloop uses that metaphor carefully. Every customer conversation should leave behind a better path: a sourced answer, a clearer doc, a safer macro, a product signal, an eval case, or a policy improvement.

The "loop" is the product truth:

1. A customer asks for help.
2. Cairnloop attaches safe app context.
3. It retrieves grounded knowledge.
4. It drafts, answers, or escalates.
5. The operator resolves the issue.
6. The conversation becomes knowledge, metrics, and product signal.
7. The next customer has a better path.

### 1.2 Brand promise

Cairnloop helps small SaaS teams answer customers without losing the knowledge hidden in support work.

### 1.3 One-line positioning

**Embedded support automation for Phoenix apps.**

### 1.4 Expanded positioning

Cairnloop is a Phoenix-native support automation layer that gives solo SaaS founders and small teams an embedded inbox, knowledge base, AI drafting, self-service answers, policy-gated automation, and customer-signal extraction - all inside the app they already operate.

### 1.5 Product posture

Cairnloop is:

- Embedded, not standalone.
- Host-owned, not black-box.
- Ecto-native, not external CRM-first.
- Knowledge-centered, not ticket-volume-centered.
- Safe-by-default, not autonomous-by-default.
- Operator-grade, not call-center cosplay.
- Useful without AI, better with carefully traced AI.

Cairnloop is not:

- An Intercom/Zendesk clone.
- A generic shared inbox.
- A chatbot-only widget.
- A full CRM.
- A marketing automation suite.
- A social inbox/contact center.
- An unrestricted AI agent.

### 1.6 Primary users

**Primary operator:** a solo SaaS founder or small technical team that needs support to be responsive, safe, and product-informative without running a separate support organization.

**Secondary operator:** a Phoenix app team that wants an embedded support cockpit and an OSS alternative to bolting on a standalone support SaaS.

**End customer:** a SaaS user who wants a direct answer, a quick human path, and confidence that the support system knows the relevant app context.

### 1.7 Brand personality

Cairnloop is a careful trail guide, a practical maintainer, and a reliability-minded operator.

It is:

- Calm, not sleepy.
- Warm, not cute.
- Technical, not sterile.
- Precise, not academic.
- Protective, not fearful.
- Opinionated, not controlling.
- OSS-native, not corporate open-core theater.

### 1.8 The five brand principles

1. **Leave a better path.** A support interaction should improve the system for the next person.
2. **Show your sources.** Customer-visible answers and operator suggestions must expose where they came from.
3. **Never trap the customer.** Self-service must include a visible human handoff path.
4. **Make safety explicit.** Risk, confidence, policy decisions, and automation levels should be visible, not hidden.
5. **Keep the app owner in control.** The host app owns auth, data, policy, context, and side effects.

## 2. Competitive distinctiveness

### 2.1 Current nearby space

The space around Cairnloop is crowded with language like helpdesk, live chat, shared inbox, AI agent, customer service platform, and omnichannel support. Chatwoot positions around open-source customer support and all customer conversations in one place; Intercom currently emphasizes an AI-agent-era helpdesk; Plain emphasizes API-first support infrastructure; Pylon emphasizes AI-native B2B support, knowledge gaps, account intelligence, and omnichannel context; Zammad is a web-based open-source support/ticketing solution; Papercups is an Elixir live customer support app but is in maintenance mode.

Cairnloop should not visually or verbally drift into their lanes.

### 2.2 How to stay distinct

Avoid these category signals:

- Bright SaaS blue as the primary action color.
- Green as the entire brand identity.
- Purple/blue AI gradients.
- Chat bubble logo shapes.
- Robot mascots.
- Headset/call-center photography.
- Generic "AI resolves everything" claims.
- Enterprise contact-center dashboards.
- A brand voice built around speed, scale, and automation rate only.

Use these Cairnloop signals instead:

- Warm technical earth palette: basalt, trailpaper, stone, lichen, copper.
- Trail-marker and evidence-card metaphors.
- Topographic paths, route marks, small stacked forms, source trails.
- Copy that emphasizes safe loops: ask, retrieve, draft, cite, hand off, improve.
- Operator language: policy, trace, source, context, handoff, gap, signal.
- OSS installation and integration patterns in hero material.

## 4. Messaging architecture

### 4.1 Message house

**Core claim:** Cairnloop makes customer support a durable product loop, not just an inbox.

Supporting claims:

1. **Embedded in your Phoenix app.** Mount the dashboard, connect your repo, and keep auth/context under host control.
2. **Knowledge is the automation substrate.** Answers come from maintained sources, not vague bot confidence.
3. **Safe automation by default.** Draft first, cite sources, gate risky actions, and never hide handoff.
4. **Support becomes signal.** Repeated questions become knowledge gaps, product friction, roadmap inputs, and reliability signals.
5. **Built for OSS maintainers.** Clear behaviours, migrations, telemetry, docs, and adapter contracts.

### 4.2 Product story

Bad support systems end at resolution. Cairnloop starts there.

A customer asks a question. Cairnloop captures the app context, finds the best sources, drafts a reply, and shows the operator what it used. If the answer is safe and grounded, it can help the customer immediately. If not, it hands off with the context intact. When the conversation is resolved, Cairnloop looks for the durable thing the team should keep: an article, a macro, an eval case, a product signal, or a policy improvement.

## 5. Voice and tone

### 5.1 Voice pillars

**Grounded.** Say what the library does, what it does not do, and what the operator controls. Do not overpromise.

**Kind.** The voice should respect both the customer who needs help and the founder/operator who is overloaded.

**Explicit.** Make hidden decisions visible: source coverage, confidence, policy, risk, handoff, trace, audit.

**Practical.** Prefer installable examples and next steps over abstract product poetry.

**Durable.** Treat support as product memory, not disposable chat.

### 5.2 Tone by surface

| Surface | Tone | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Landing page | confident, warm, concise | "Every conversation can improve the next answer." |
| README/docs | precise, maintainer-friendly | "Run the installer, mount the dashboard, then review the generated policy module." |
| Admin UI | calm, action-oriented | "Draft ready. Source coverage: 3 articles. Policy: human review." |
| End-user widget | helpful, non-blocking | "I found a likely answer. If this is not right, I can send it to support with your context." |
| Errors | accountable, plain | "Cairnloop could not send the reply because the email adapter returned an error." |
| AI safety | transparent, not scary | "This reply is blocked because it mentions billing and has no approved source." |
| OSS community | humble, collaborative | "This adapter contract is intentionally small; open an issue if your channel needs a new callback." |

### 5.3 Writing rules

1. Start with the user's goal.
2. Name the system state.
3. Explain why when a decision is blocked or escalated.
4. Use verbs that reveal the loop: ingest, retrieve, draft, cite, approve, hand off, resolve, mark, promote.
5. Prefer "conversation" over "ticket."
6. Prefer "operator" over "agent" for humans using the admin UI.
7. Use "AI" as a capability, not a character.
8. Never imply the AI is sentient, caring, or independently authorized.
9. Do not apologize on behalf of the user. Do apologize when the system failed.
10. Keep customer-visible messages short and easy to escape.

### 5.4 Word bank

Use often:

- trail, marker, source, path, loop, handoff, context, grounded, durable, signal, gap, policy, evidence, trace, route, review, publish, promote, resolve, cite, safe, host-owned.

Use carefully:

- automation, AI, agent, deflection, risk, confidence, eval, triage, cockpit, substrate, intelligence.

Avoid or ban:

- magical, autonomous, omniscient, bot army, ticket crushing, call center, replace support, hallucination-proof, zero-touch, human-like, delight at scale, growth hack, AI employee.

### 5.5 UX microcopy examples

Admin inbox states:

- New conversation
- Waiting on operator
- Waiting on customer
- Waiting on approval
- Sourced answer ready
- Draft needs review
- Policy blocked
- Knowledge gap marked
- Promoted to article draft
- Signal cluster updated

AI panel:

- "Draft created from 3 published sources."
- "This answer is draft-only because the intent is billing."
- "No safe source found. Ask a clarifying question or hand off."
- "The customer has already seen two self-service answers. Handoff is recommended."
- "This tool call requires approval before any customer-visible action."

End-user widget:

- "I found a likely answer from the docs."
- "Want me to send this to support with your account context attached?"
- "This looks account-specific, so I will route it to a person."
- "You will not need to repeat what you already typed."

Blocked automation:

- "Blocked: no approved source covers this claim."
- "Blocked: refund language requires operator approval."
- "Blocked: customer identity could not be verified for this action."

### 5.6 Error message pattern

Use this shape:

1. What happened.
2. Why it matters.
3. What to do next.

Example:

> Cairnloop could not deliver this reply because the email adapter returned `:rate_limited`. The conversation is still saved and the draft is intact. Retry delivery or switch the conversation to manual follow-up.

## 6. Visual identity

### 6.1 Visual thesis

Cairnloop should look like an operator-grade trail system: quiet, durable, source-aware, and warm. The interface should feel like a well-kept field notebook crossed with a reliability dashboard.

Avoid shiny SaaS glass, generic AI gradients, and large happy-human support photos. Use stone, route, evidence, and topographic language.

### 6.2 Logo direction

Recommended primary mark:

A compact cairn made of 3-4 irregular rounded stone forms, with a subtle loop/path line that wraps behind or through the stack. The loop should imply feedback and routing, not infinity-symbol futurism.

Geometry rules:

- Use asymmetric stone widths. Perfect symmetry feels generic.
- Keep the base stone widest and calm.
- Keep the loop line thin and secondary.
- Never make the mark look like a chat bubble.
- Never make the mark look like a yoga/wellness rock stack.
- Never use a robot face, headset, or speech balloon inside the mark.

### 6.3 Wordmark

Use a custom or lightly customized wordmark built from the chosen sans family. Recommended spelling in the wordmark: **cairnloop** lowercase. In prose, use **Cairnloop**.

Recommended wordmark traits:

- Single word, no camel-case in marketing.
- Slightly tightened tracking.
- Rounded enough to feel human, not bubbly.
- The `r`, `n`, and `l` should remain highly legible.
- Consider a subtle custom `oo` treatment that suggests the loop, but do not turn the wordmark into an infinity logo.

### 6.4 Logo lockups

1. **Primary horizontal:** cairn mark + `cairnloop` wordmark.
2. **Vertical:** mark above wordmark for README cards, social, slides.
3. **Icon only:** cairn mark for favicon, package avatar, app sidebar.
4. **Monochrome:** Basalt on Trailpaper, Trailpaper on Basalt.
5. **One-color print:** Basalt only.

Clearspace:

- Minimum clearspace equals the height of the top stone in the mark.
- Do not place the mark inside a speech bubble, pill, or generic app icon shape unless required by platform constraints.

Minimum sizes:

- Icon mark: 24 px digital minimum, 16 px favicon simplified version.
- Horizontal lockup: 112 px minimum width digital.
- Print: 0.35 in minimum icon height.

### 6.5 Shape language

Use:

- Irregular rounded rectangles and stone-like capsules.
- Thin route lines.
- Topographic contour lines.
- Small waymark dots.
- Evidence cards with visible source edges.
- Layered panels that feel like stacked notes.

Avoid:

- Speech bubbles as core shapes.
- Floating 3D bots.
- Neon AI sparkles.
- Perfectly round blob illustrations.
- Overly literal mountains everywhere.
- Emoji-heavy product UI.

## 8. Typography

### 8.1 Recommended type stack

**Primary UI and docs:** Atkinson Hyperlegible Next, with Atkinson Hyperlegible fallback. Use it because Cairnloop's product must be readable in dense admin UI, code-adjacent docs, and customer support surfaces.

**Marketing display accent:** Fraunces Soft, used sparingly for homepage hero headlines, pull quotes, or one-word section openers. Use optical sizing. Keep the "wonk" axis restrained. This creates a warmer, more human visual voice than generic SaaS sans-only systems.

**Code and metrics:** Martian Mono or Atkinson Hyperlegible Mono. Use for code snippets, telemetry names, eval IDs, trace spans, and small dashboard metrics. Martian Mono is more distinctive; Atkinson Mono is more legibility-aligned.

**Fallback stack:**

```css
--cl-font-sans: "Atkinson Hyperlegible Next", "Atkinson Hyperlegible", ui-sans-serif, system-ui, -apple-system, BlinkMacSystemFont, "Segoe UI", sans-serif;
--cl-font-display: "Fraunces", "Atkinson Hyperlegible Next", Georgia, serif;
--cl-font-mono: "Martian Mono", "Atkinson Hyperlegible Mono", ui-monospace, SFMono-Regular, Menlo, Monaco, Consolas, monospace;
```

## 9. Imagery and art direction

### 9.1 Imagery thesis

Cairnloop imagery should express quiet wayfinding and durable evidence. The best images feel like a marker on a trail, a close-up of lichen on stone, a map with a route, or an operator's field notebook - not a customer service commercial.

### 9.2 Approved image categories

1. **Trail markers and cairns** - close crops, lichen, basalt, granite, soft weather, low saturation.
2. **Topographic and route imagery** - contour lines, route marks, path overlays, hand-drawn wayfinding.
3. **Material textures** - stone, paper, moss, copper, graphite, weathered labels.
4. **Operator workspaces** - minimal desk, terminal, notebook, docs, but no staged call-center scenes.
5. **Product UI composites** - real UI cards over subtle trailpaper/contour backgrounds.
6. **Abstract support loops** - small markers connected by route lines, not glowing AI networks.

### 9.3 Forbidden imagery

- Headset support agents smiling at camera.
- Call center rows.
- Handshake stock photos.
- Chat bubble swarms.
- 3D robots, android faces, holographic AI brains.
- Blue/purple neural-network backgrounds.
- Wellness/spa pebble stacks.
- Mystical runes or fantasy maps.
- Extreme mountaineering hero shots that make the product feel rugged/outdoor rather than embedded software.
- Overly cute illustrations of people in oversized SaaS dashboards.

### 9.4 Photo treatment

- Crop close. The cairn/marker should be tactile, not scenic tourism.
- Reduce saturation by 8-15 percent.
- Add subtle Trailpaper warmth, not sepia.
- Use Basalt text overlays only on light or darkened areas with sufficient contrast.
- Overlay route lines sparingly in Path Copper.
- Prefer shallow depth of field or quiet negative space for hero imagery.
- Do not use photos where the cairn is artificially stacked in a way that suggests environmental harm or wellness aesthetics.

### 9.5 Illustration style

- 2 px strokes, rounded caps, slightly irregular geometry.
- Icons should feel like map symbols and operator controls.
- Use filled "stone" shapes only for emphasis.
- Use route lines to connect panels or explain flow.
- Avoid cartoon humans unless truly necessary.

| Blocked | route closed marker |

## 10. UI design system direction

### 10.1 Product UI thesis

The Cairnloop admin UI should feel like an operator cockpit, not a generic CRUD admin panel. The operator should always see: what happened, what the system knows, what it used, what it recommends, what is risky, and what the next safe action is.

### 10.2 Layout pattern

Recommended inbox layout:

- Left rail: queues, states, saved views, backlog health.
- Center panel: conversation timeline and reply composer.
- Right panel: customer context, AI draft, sources, policy result, suggested actions.
- Bottom/right drawer: trace/eval details when needed.

### 10.3 Core components

**Conversation row**

- Customer/account name.
- Intent chip.
- State chip.
- Last message preview.
- Source/handoff indicators.
- Risk badge when applicable.
- Age/backlog marker.

**Source card**

- Article title.
- Version.
- Audience scope.
- Snippet.
- Last published date.
- Used by draft? yes/no.
- Confidence/evidence indicator.

**Policy gate card**

- Decision: allow, draft only, require approval, deny.
- Reason.
- Risk level.
- Required operator action.
- Trace link.

**Knowledge gap card**

- Title.
- Evidence count.
- Related conversations.
- Suggested article title.
- Severity.
- Last seen.
- Actions: promote to draft, ignore, merge, assign.

**Signal cluster card**

- Product area.
- Pattern summary.
- Affected accounts/users.
- Related deploy/flag/incident when available.
- Suggested owner.

### 10.4 Component styling

- Cards: Warm Stone surfaces, 1 px Granite border, 12 px radius.
- Active cards: 2 px Path Copper left route marker or top marker, not full orange fill.
- Buttons: Path Copper primary, Basalt secondary, transparent tertiary.
- Badges: muted surface fills with clear text; never rely on color alone.
- Focus: 2 px Path Copper ring with 2 px offset.
- Shadows: minimal. Prefer borders and elevation through layers.
- Motion: small route-progress transitions; no bouncy chatbot animation.

### 10.5 Micro-interaction principles

- Confirm customer-visible side effects.
- Auto-save drafts and say so quietly.
- Show policy reasons inline.
- Keep escalation visible.
- Never animate in a way that implies the AI is thinking like a person.
- Use progress labels: "retrieving sources," "checking policy," "draft ready." Do not use "AI is pondering."

### 10.6 State chips

| State | Label | Color role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| New | New | Info |
| Open | Open | Copper |
| Waiting on customer | Waiting on customer | Slate |
| Waiting on operator | Waiting on operator | Ember |
| Waiting on approval | Approval needed | Heather + Ember |
| Resolved | Resolved | Lichen |
| Closed | Closed | Slate |
| Spam | Spam | Fault Clay |
| Policy blocked | Blocked | Fault Clay |
| Knowledge gap | Gap marked | Heather |

## 11. Documentation style

### 11.1 Docs principle

Docs should feel like a maintainer guiding another maintainer through a safe install. Be explicit about ownership, generated files, migrations, security, adapter boundaries, and what is not automatic.

### 11.2 Docs structure

1. What Cairnloop does.
2. What it does not own.
3. Install.
4. Mount dashboard.
5. Configure repo and auth.
6. Add context provider.
7. Add policy module.
8. Add first inbox/channel.
9. Create first KB article.
10. Enable draft mode.
11. Review telemetry and traces.
12. Move to self-service mode.
13. Safe automation checklist.
14. Adapter authoring.
15. Security and privacy.
16. Upgrade/migration guide.

### 11.3 Documentation voice examples

Good:

> Cairnloop does not make authorization decisions for your app. The dashboard runs inside your Phoenix router and should be protected by your normal browser/admin pipelines.

Bad:

> Cairnloop magically integrates with your auth.

Good:

> The default automation mode is draft-only. Customer-visible AI replies require an explicit policy decision.

Bad:

> Turn on AI and let Cairnloop handle support for you.

### 11.4 Code example tone

Use realistic Phoenix examples. Avoid toy names like `Acme` except in docs where generic code is required. Show host ownership clearly.

Recommended module names:

```elixir
defmodule MyApp.Support.ContextProvider do
  @behaviour CairnLoop.ContextProvider
end

defmodule MyApp.Support.Policy do
  @behaviour CairnLoop.AutomationPolicy
end
```

### 11.5 Readability rules for docs

- Lead with a practical outcome.
- Use short paragraphs.
- Use tables for options and callbacks.
- Document defaults and why they exist.
- Always include a "safe default" note for automation features.
- Include failure modes.
- Include "how to verify" sections after install steps.

## 13. Brand-specific UX copy system

### 13.1 Automation level names

Use clear names, not cute branded names.

| Level | UI label | Description |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 0 | Off | Store and route only |
| 1 | Draft-only | AI drafts; operator sends |
| 2 | Sourced self-service | Widget answers from approved sources with handoff |
| 3 | Low-risk auto-reply | Send only when sourced and policy allows |
| 4 | Tool proposal | AI suggests action; operator approves |
| 5 | Narrow automatic tool | Allowlisted side effects only |
| 6 | Broad autonomy | Not a default Cairnloop mode |

### 13.2 Policy decision copy

- Allow: "Allowed by policy."
- Draft only: "Draft only. Review before sending."
- Require approval: "Approval required before customer-visible action."
- Deny: "Blocked by policy."

Reason examples:

- "No approved source covers this answer."
- "The conversation is about billing."
- "The draft makes a promise about future product behavior."
- "The customer identity is not verified for this action."
- "The retrieval score is below your self-service threshold."

### 13.3 Knowledge gap copy

- "Mark as gap"
- "Promote to article draft"
- "Merge with existing gap"
- "Ignore for now"
- "17 conversations match this pattern"
- "No published article covers this question"

### 13.4 Handoff copy

End-user:

> This looks like something a person should review. I can send it to support with the details you already shared.

Operator:

> Handoff created. The customer saw two suggested answers. Neither was marked helpful. Current page, account plan, and attempted sources are attached.

## 15. Motion and interaction

### 15.1 Motion principles

- Motion should clarify route, state, or progress.
- Keep transitions under 180 ms for UI state changes.
- Use longer 240-320 ms only for panel reveals.
- Prefer opacity + small translate. Avoid elastic motion.
- Respect reduced-motion preferences.

### 15.2 Approved motion motifs

- A route line drawing between steps in a loop diagram.
- A small marker moving from retrieve -> draft -> policy -> review.
- Source cards stacking in as evidence is retrieved.
- A policy gate changing from pending to draft-only/blocked/allowed.

### 15.3 Forbidden motion motifs

- Typing indicator for AI unless a real streamed response is being displayed.
- Robot thinking animation.
- Sparkle explosions.
- Infinite pulsing around AI cards.
- Confetti for resolved support conversations.

## 16. Accessibility and inclusion

### 16.1 Accessibility posture

Cairnloop's brand must reflect its product promise: support should reduce effort. Visual and UX decisions should minimize cognitive load, support keyboard navigation, and expose state clearly.

### 16.2 Requirements

- Meet WCAG 2.2 AA for text and UI components.
- Prefer AAA for docs body text.
- Do not rely on color alone.
- Provide visible focus states.
- Use alt text for all informational imagery.
- Keep decorative texture behind content low contrast.
- Respect dark mode and reduced motion.
- Write end-user widget copy at a simple reading level.

### 16.3 Alt text style

Good:

> A lichen-covered stone cairn marking a quiet mountain trail.

Bad:

> Beautiful calming rock stack with peaceful vibes.

For product screenshots:

> Cairnloop admin UI showing a conversation, sourced draft, and policy decision panel.

## 17. Brand governance

### 17.1 Do and do not

Do:

- Use the Cairnloop name consistently.
- Use `CairnLoop` only in code.
- Use Path Copper sparingly for action and route.
- Keep AI decisions explainable.
- Show sources and policy states.
- Use trail/cairn metaphors with restraint.

Do not:

- Use chat bubbles as the logo.
- Use bright blue as the primary identity.
- Use green as the primary action color.
- Use robot imagery.
- Call it an AI agent product.
- Claim it replaces humans.
- Hide handoff.
- Use generic helpdesk stock photos.

### 17.2 Trademark/package caution

Initial web searches found "Cairn Loop" mostly as trail names and outdoor references, plus unrelated "Cairn" and "Loop" businesses. That is encouraging for distinctiveness in the support/software category, but it is not a legal clearance. Before publishing widely, check at minimum:

- Hex package availability for `cairn_loop`.
- GitHub organization/repository availability.
- npm package if a JS widget package is planned.
- Domain availability.
- USPTO, EUIPO, UKIPO, and relevant trademark classes.
- Existing OSS repos with similar names.

## 18. Implementation tokens

Use the included `cairnloop.tokens.json` and `cairnloop.css` files as the starting point for design/dev implementation.

Token naming principle: primitive tokens describe what the color is; semantic tokens describe what the color does.

Primitive:

```css
--cl-color-basalt: #18211F;
```

Semantic:

```css
--cl-bg: var(--cl-color-trailpaper);
```

Component:

```css
--cl-button-primary-bg: var(--cl-primary);
```

## 19. Quick LLM rules for future writing agents

When writing for Cairnloop:

1. Present Cairnloop as an embedded Phoenix support automation library, not a SaaS helpdesk.
2. Use the tagline "Support that leaves a trail" unless a page needs a more specific technical headline.
3. Emphasize: host-owned, Ecto-native, sourced answers, safe handoff, policy-gated automation, knowledge gaps, customer signals.
4. Avoid: chatbot, AI agent replacement, helpdesk clone, ticket crushing, omnichannel suite, CRM, marketing automation.
5. Use calm, precise, practical language.
6. When describing AI, mention sources, confidence, policy, audit, and handoff.
7. When writing UI copy, show the state and next safe action.
8. Use the visual words: trailpaper, basalt, granite, lichen, copper, route, marker, source, evidence, loop.
9. Never make the product sound magical or fully autonomous.
10. Keep the human handoff visible.
